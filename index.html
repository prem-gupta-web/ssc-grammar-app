Rule 1: Use of Since and For

  • Since: Used for a specific point of time (e.g., since 2002, since morning).

Rule 2: Each / Every / Either / Neither / Any / None

  • Each: Functions as both an Adjective and Pronoun. Can act directly as a subject. Takes a singular verb.
  • Every: Functions only as an Adjective. Cannot act directly as a subject (e.g., write Every one of instead of Every of). Takes a singular verb.
  • Either / Neither: Used strictly for comparing or making a choice between two items or people.
  • Any / None: Used for choices involving more than two items or people.

Rule 3: Relative Pronouns (Who / Whom / Whose / Which / That)

  • Who: Functions as a Subject pronoun (used for persons).
  • Whom: Functions as an Object pronoun (following a verb or preposition).
  • Whose: Shows possession or ownership.
  • Who's: Contraction of Who is, Who was, or Who has.

Rule 4: Modal Verbs and Superfluous Overlaps

  • Modal verbs (can, could, may, might, shall, should, will, would, must) take the base form of the verb (V1).
  • Avoid using redundancy like can/could with be able to, or may/might with possibly/likely.
  • Lest is always followed by should (and cannot take a negative word like not).
  • Had better is followed by a bare infinitive (V1 without to).

Rule 5: Singular and Plural Nouns

  • Certain nouns are always uncountable and singular; they do not take a plural ending (-s/-es) and take a singular verb (e.g., furniture, advice, information, luggage, poetry, work, scenery).
  • Certain collective nouns are always plural in concept and take plural verbs, even without an -s (e.g., cattle, poultry, gentry, vermin).
  • Compound nouns form plurals by modifying the root/principal word (e.g., mothers-in-law, passers-by).

Rule 6: Infinitives and Bare Infinitives

  • Standard verbs take the full infinitive (to + V1).
  • Causative and sensory verbs like let, make, see, watch, hear, bid take a bare infinitive (V1 without to) in the active voice.
  • In the passive voice, make takes the full infinitive (to + V1), while let retains its bare form or structural alternative.
